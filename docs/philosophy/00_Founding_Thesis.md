> **Touch is not a transaction. It is a language.**

## Why Litmo exists

Litmo exists because safe, meaningful, platonic touch is a real human need, yet adults have almost no shared language, structure, or social infrastructure for asking for it safely.

The Constitution defines what Litmo must never betray. Product requirements define what Litmo should do. Architecture and code define how it works. This founding thesis comes before all of them: it names the human problem the project was created to address and the principle from which the rest of the system follows.

If Litmo succeeds over decades, it will not be because a feed was optimized. It will be because a generation inherited a **clearer grammar for consent** than the one we were given.

---

## The problem

Loneliness does not live only in the mind. It also lives in the body.

People can be surrounded by others and still go without safe, affirming physical contact for months or years. That absence can be especially acute for trauma survivors, autistic and otherwise neurodivergent people, widowed people, veterans, students far from home, disabled people navigating inaccessible social spaces, elders, and anyone whose need for closeness does not fit neatly into romance, sex, family, or paid care.

The common advice to “just hug someone” ignores the real difficulty of the request. Adults are rarely taught how to name a need for touch without implying sexual or romantic intent. They are rarely given a framework for specifying pressure, duration, body boundaries, environment, or an immediate exit. Where no shared grammar exists, people rely on assumption. **Assumption is where confusion, coercion, shame, and harm begin.**

Across cultures, the *form* of touch differs — but the need for **specificity and exit** does not disappear. A grammar that only fits one culture’s norms becomes soft colonialism. A grammar that refuses stop words becomes violence with better UX.

## The central insight

Touch is a language.

Like any language, it has grammar:

- pressure;  
- duration;  
- location;  
- movement;  
- environment;  
- emotional context;  
- intention;  
- time;  
- and the ability to stop.  

Different forms of touch communicate different things. A brief hand on a shoulder is not the same sentence as a firm, sustained hold in silence. Neither can be interpreted safely without mutual understanding.

Litmo's central design claim is therefore simple:

> The problem is not that touch is inherently unsafe. The problem is that we have never built a sufficiently explicit shared grammar for it.

If the missing grammar can be built, then people can express what they need, understand what another person can offer, confirm the exact overlap, and withdraw at any time without penalty.

### Living language (thesis extension)

Languages that matter become **living**:

1. **Versioned** — words and seals have versions and fingerprints.  
2. **Enforceable** — stop words work in code, not only in manners.  
3. **Dialectical** — plain language, ND accommodations, and cultural localization translate without erasing Soft Signal.  
4. **Amendable** — vocabulary grows through constitutional process, not silent product drift.  
5. **Intergenerational** — what we teach about silence and stop outlives any release train.  

Consent is not a one-time checkbox. It is a living, session-specific, dual-party protocol with free revocation.

## What Litmo is building

Litmo is not a dating app, therapy platform, or marketplace for paid touch. It is consent-centered infrastructure for safe, non-sexual, platonic physical connection between adults.

Its core concepts follow directly from the founding thesis:

### Touch Language Profile

A person should be able to describe their relationship to touch before becoming vulnerable with another person. Preferences such as hold type, pressure, duration, body zones, environment, and nervous-system context must be expressible in clear language — including soft limits as first-class care zones.

### Consent Snapshot

Consent must be specific to the present interaction. Before a session begins, both people should review and affirm the exact intersection of what each person has said they want and can offer. A match, past session, profile, or trust history never substitutes for present consent. Seals are fingerprint-bound and invalidate on material change.

### Soft Signal

Every interaction must include an immediate, understandable exit. Stopping must not require persuasion, justification, or social negotiation. A person should be able to say, in effect, “I need this to stop,” and have that boundary honored completely — offline-first, unilateral, non-punitive.

### Continuous consent (second-level grammar)

During a session, authority is not frozen forever by the seal alone. Continuous evaluation, check-ins, and revocation masks exist so that silence is not renewal and freeze is not yes. Soft Signal remains Level-0 free.

### Trust Ledger

Trust should be gradual, transparent, and earned through repeated conduct. A record may support accountability, but it must never certify that a person is universally safe or override current boundaries.

### Collective safety (second-order)

Blocks, reports, human moderation, appeals, and holds protect communities. They never replace Soft Signal, never auto-punish for engagement convenience, and never create public shame scores.

### Neurodivergent & trauma accommodations (second-order)

Pace, sensory density, reduced motion, language preference, and overload exits are first-class product logic — device-local, not diagnoses, not partner-visible badges.

## Second-order principles (systems around the person)

### Collective safety without erasing the individual

Communities need tools. Individuals keep absolute stop rights. Human review for irreversible restrictions. No safety marketplace.

### Cultural adoption without monoculture

Litmo spreads **specificity and exit**, not one culture’s ideal of intimacy. Localization must preserve stop freeness and dual seal.

### Living language governance

New vocabulary requires docs, tests, dual-party meaning, and fail-closed defaults. Clients share domain engines so consent law does not fork.

## Third-order principles (civilizational horizon)

### Cultural literacy over engagement

The fifty-year win is people speaking clearer boundaries offline — not more minutes in an app.

### Intergenerational duty

Design that trains silence-as-yes or stop-as-shame is a civilizational failure, not a growth experiment.

### Resistance to capture

Engagement capitalism that monetizes isolation is an existential threat to the thesis. Softness is not a funnel.

### Continuity of care for data

Consent data obligations outlive founders, funding rounds, and product pivots.

## Edge cases the thesis must survive

| Edge | Constitutional response |
| ---- | ------------------------ |
| Freeze / fawn / shutdown | Not a yes; Soft Signal free; no required narrative |
| Cross-cultural touch norms | Grammar + exit, not one “correct” culture |
| Neurotype differences | ND Mode accommodations; never public traits |
| Offline / loss of connectivity | Local stop authoritative; fail closed on seal |
| Power imbalance / coercion | Strictest wins; report/block paths; no auto-punish theater |
| Institutional adoption | No surveillance wellness; adult voluntary use only |
| AI assistance | Never infers consent or diagnoses users |

## Product implications

### Consent must be product logic

Consent cannot exist only in disclaimers, policies, or educational copy. It must shape data models, state transitions, permissions, matching, session activation, exits, and moderation.

### Privacy must be the default

Litmo may hold intimate information about bodies, boundaries, trauma, relationships, and personal needs. That information must not become social content, advertising material, engagement fuel, or silent model context. Users must understand what is collected, who can access it, and how to delete or export it.

### Technology should prepare people for human connection, not replace it

Litmo succeeds when people become clearer, safer, and more capable of relating to one another outside the app. Time spent in the product is not the primary measure of value. The platform should support connection and then get out of the way.

### Safety outranks growth

No growth target, engagement metric, social feature, recommendation system, or business objective may weaken explicit consent, privacy, reversibility, or user agency.

### Uncertainty must remain visible

Litmo must not imply certainty it does not possess. It cannot determine that a person is safe, infer consent, diagnose a relationship, or guarantee an outcome. It can provide structure, evidence, and language while preserving the limits of what the system knows.

## The test for every feature

Before a feature belongs in Litmo, ask:

1. Does it make consent clearer?  
2. Does it preserve privacy by default?  
3. Does it increase user agency rather than pressure?  
4. Is the action understandable and reversible?  
5. Could it expose, mislead, shame, or strand someone?  
6. Does it support human connection outside the app rather than replace it?  
7. Does Soft Signal remain freer than continue under this design?  
8. Would we still ship it if it defined consent culture for fifty years?  

If the answer to those questions is not satisfactory, the feature is not ready.

---

## Founding principle

> **Every feature should make safe human connection easier, clearer, or more trustworthy. If it does not, it probably does not belong in Litmo.**

Litmo is not trying to optimize touch.

It is trying to protect it — long enough that the language outlasts the product surface.
